Introduction

The dynamics of behaviors in a population is a complex process. Nevertheless, we can attempt to formalize it with mathematical and computational models.

The goal of the model here is to formalize complex contagion dynamics on social networks. A complex contagion is a contagion of a behavior where a susceptible individual needs to be exposed to a behavior multiple times - rather than just once, as in simple contagion - before the individual adopts the behavior. There is an increasingly large body of evidence indicating that behavioral spread follows the logic of complex contagion.

Previous models of complex contagion have assumed that a new behavior spreads in the population, but that the resident behavior does not spread. Such models are rooted in epidemiological models where a infectious agent can be transmitted from host to host, but transmission always occurs in a single direction: the infectious state is contagious, but the susceptible state is not. Behaviors, however, often do not fall into this category. Behaviors can be adopted because they are communicated by others through peer influence or observational learning, but there is no reason to assume that this is true only of a novel behavior, but not of the resident behavior (there might be cases where such an assumption is justified, and the model proposed here allows to capture these cases as well). Previous models of complex contagion have thus focused on the spread of a new behavior, but have always implicitly assumed that the new behavior will spread, and have thus mainly been concerned with questions about the speed at which a new behavior can spread.

I will be using a population memetics approach where by meme I mean any culture entity that can be stably transmitted.
